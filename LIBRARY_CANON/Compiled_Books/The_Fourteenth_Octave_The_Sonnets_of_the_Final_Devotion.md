## II. SONNET-BY-SONNET GEOMETRIC ANALYSIS

### Sonnet 105: "Let not my love be called idolatry"
**Phase**: Defense against idolatry charge
**Geometric Issue**: Is the Poet's worship of the Youth blasphemous?

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Accusation**: Treating the Youth as divine = idolatry
- **The Defense**: "One thing expressing, leaves out difference"
- **The Argument**: The Poet doesn't worship multiple gods—only one subject (monotheism defense)
- **The Trinity**: "Fair, kind, and true" = three attributes of one entity
- **The Formula**: "Three themes in one, which wondrous scope affords"
- **Translation**: Like Christian Trinity (Father, Son, Holy Spirit = One God), the Youth's attributes are unified
- This is **Theological Mathematics**—using sacred geometry to justify secular devotion

### Sonnet 106: "When in the chronicle of wasted time"
**Phase**: Historical precedent argument
**Geometric Issue**: Has anyone described perfect beauty before?

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Research**: Reading old texts describing ancient beauties
- **The Observation**: "Even such a beauty as you master now"
- **The Problem**: Old poets lacked adequate terms ("they lacked the skill")
- **The Claim**: Historical beauties were **Prefiguring Types** of the Youth
- **The Conclusion**: "For we, which now behold these present days / Have eyes to wonder, but lack tongues to praise"
- **Translation**: The Youth is what all previous beauties predicted
- This is **Typological Interpretation**—reading all history as foreshadowing one culmination

### Sonnet 107: "Not mine own fears, nor the prophetic soul"
**Phase**: Defying prophecy / Survival against prediction
**Geometric Issue**: The relationship survived despite predicted doom

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Context**: "The mortal moon hath her eclipse endured"
- **Interpretation**: Possible references to Queen Elizabeth's death (1603) or military events
- **The Claim**: "My love looks fresh, and Death to me subscribes"
- **The Defiance**: "Since, spite of him, I'll live in this poor rhyme"
- **The Immortality Formula**: Verse outlives prophecy
- **System Status**: Against all predictions, the thread persists
- This is **Survival Bias**—celebrating persistence without analyzing sustainability

### Sonnet 108: "What's in the brain that ink may character"
**Phase**: Justification for repetition
**Geometric Issue**: Why does the Poet keep writing the same thing?

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Question**: "What's new to speak, what new to register?"
- **The Answer**: "Nothing, sweet boy; but yet, like prayers divine / I must each day say o'er the very same"
- **The Comparison**: Religious prayer = repeating identical words daily
- **The Function**: Not novelty but **Ritual Maintenance**
- **The Claim**: "Counting no old thing old, thou mine, I thine"
- **Translation**: Repetition doesn't indicate staleness—it indicates reliability
- This is **Protocol Stability**—arguing that unchanging message proves constancy

### Sonnet 109: "O, never say that I was false of heart"
**Phase**: Defense against inconstancy charge
**Geometric Issue**: Has the Poet's devotion wavered?

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Accusation**: The Poet has strayed ("ranged")
- **The Defense**: "Just to the time, not with the time exchanged"
- **Translation**: "I left temporarily but didn't replace you"
- **The Explanation**: "I have hoisted sail to all the winds / Which should transport me farthest from your sight"
- **The Reality**: But all paths return: "Save where you are how happy you make those"
- **The Formula**: All vectors eventually resolve back to origin (the Youth)
- This is **Homeostatic Return**—claiming all deviations are temporary perturbations

### Sonnet 110: "Alas, 'tis true I have gone here and there"
**Phase**: Admission of infidelity with mitigation
**Geometric Issue**: The Poet admits "straying" but seeks forgiveness

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Confession**: "And made myself a motley to the view"
- **Translation**: "I made a fool of myself publicly"
- **The Admission**: "Gored mine own thoughts, sold cheap what is most dear"
- **The Claim**: These were "experiments" to test the Youth's value
- **The Conclusion**: "Now all is done, have what shall have no end"
- **The Math**: Past infidelities → Present understanding → Future constancy
- This is **Trial-and-Error Justification**—framing betrayal as research

### Sonnet 111: "O, for my sake do you with Fortune chide"
**Phase**: Environmental determinism / Blame transfer
**Geometric Issue**: Are the Poet's failures Fortune's fault?

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Excuse**: "The guilty goddess of my harmful deeds"
- **Translation**: Fortune made me do it
- **The Mechanism**: "That did not better for my life provide / Than public means which public manners breeds"
- **The Claim**: Low social status → Bad behavior (environmental determinism)
- **The Request**: "Pity me then, and wish I were renewed"
- **The Plea**: Send corrective "medicine" (the Youth's influence)
- **System Diagnosis**: External locus of control—the Poet is not responsible
- This is **Agency Denial**—attributing choices to circumstances

### Sonnet 112: "Your love and pity doth the impression fill"
**Phase**: Selective attention / Reputation management
**Geometric Issue**: Should the Poet care about public opinion?

**Engineering Assessment**:
- **The Claim**: The Youth's approval is the only metric that matters
- **The Shut-Down**: "You are my all the world, and I must strive / To know my shames and praises from your tongue"
- **The Conclusion**: "None else to me, nor I to none alive"
- **Translation**: All external observers are irrelevant
- **The Problem**: "In so profound abysm I throw all care / Of others' voices"
- **The Risk**: Complete isolation in a two-person system
- This is **Dyadic Enclosure**—severing all external reference points
