# THE LESION
## Book 5: The Transfinite Horizon — Chapter 6: Perceptual Surgery

Kaelen found the man — once a foreman in the Archon's brick kilns — in a corner of the newly rendered meadow. He wasn't planting seeds. He was frantically scooping up the dark, rich compost meant for the vines and piling it into a mound, smoothing the sides with shaking hands.

"Look at it," the foreman whispered, his eyes red-rimmed and weeping. "The first vault. We'll fire these blocks by noon. We'll be safe. We'll have the gold again."

Kaelen looked at the "gold." It was steaming dung and wet silt. To a clear eye, it was fertiliser. To the foreman, it was a bank.

"Barak," Kaelen said softly, kneeling beside him. "It's just waste. You're building a cage out of your own exhaust."

"You want it for yourself!" Barak shrieked, shielding the pile. He turned to look at Kaelen, and Kaelen flinched. Barak's left eye was nearly swollen shut. A thick, white, calcified lesion sat squarely over the pupil — a physical Sty.

"It hurts to look at you," Barak whimpered, the light of the sun causing him to shiver. "You're too bright. Everything is too sharp. The old City was soft. The Archon gave us the shade."

"The Archon gave you a cache because your eyes were too infected to do the math," Kaelen said. He reached out, slowly. "Stay still, Barak. I'm going to wash it."

"No! It's my greatness! I worked twenty years for that Log!"

Kaelen didn't argue. He moved with the speed of a Hired Man — deliberate, precise, without hesitation. He held Barak's head steady and applied a compress of wild yeast and river water — the Prime Flow, unmeasured, unprocessed, straight from the source.

As the water touched the lesion, Barak screamed. It wasn't the scream of a man being tortured; it was the scream of a man being evaluated. The white film over his eye began to dissolve, the calcified accumulation breaking down into its chemical components — twenty years of unprocessed fixation, twenty years of looking at dung and calling it gold, twenty years of exponential bias crystallised into a physical obstruction.

Kaelen wiped away the white sludge. Barak gasped, blinking rapidly. He looked down at his hands, then at the pile of dung he had been smoothing.

The illusion flickered and died. He saw the filth for what it was. He saw the "Gold" for what it was. He looked up at the meadow, and for the first time, he didn't see a flat, grey grid. He saw the irrational green of the Prime.

He began to weep — true, cleansing tears. "It's... it's too much," Barak sobbed, covering his face. "I want to go back. I want my Log back. It was easier when I couldn't see."

"The Garden is exhausting," Kaelen agreed, standing up. "But at least you're not eating the Pig anymore."

---

Barak sat for a long time after that. The dung pile steamed quietly beside him, already cooling, already becoming what it actually was — fertiliser for the vines that someone else would plant tomorrow.

Kaelen watched from across the meadow. He knew the pattern. He had seen it forty times since the door opened.

Some, like Adrik, accepted the cure. They flexed their newly unwound wrists and picked up real stones and built real walls and wept only a little.

Some, like Barak, accepted the cure but couldn't bear the brightness. They would shade their eyes for weeks. They would flinch at direct sunlight. They would reach, sometimes, for the dung pile in the early morning when nobody was watching — not because they believed it was gold anymore, but because the texture was familiar and the world was too new and the Prime was relentless in its refusal to be anything other than itself.

And some — Kaelen knew this too, and it was the knowledge that made him leave every city eventually — some would find the lesion growing back. Not because the surgery failed. Because the patient missed the Log. Because the Log had become load-bearing. Because the man who has seen the world through an exponential filter for twenty years has built his entire identity around the distortion, and removing the distortion doesn't just correct his vision — it erases his map.

A man without a map in the Garden is free.

A man without a map who remembers the City is terrified.

Kaelen couldn't fix that. Couldn't teach around it. Couldn't force the eyes to stay open. He could wash and wash and the patient could close and close and eventually the Hired Man had to accept that some people would rather be blind in a world they understood than sighted in a world that required them to start from the mud.

That wasn't failure. That was the wobble.

Even the surgery has a remainder.

He turned back to the river. Somewhere downstream, Veth was teaching someone to check an angle with their body. The sound of stone on stone carried across the water — the steady, imperfect, continuing rhythm of the pragmatic straight.

Straight enough. Keep moving.

Even when they walk back into the Sty.
