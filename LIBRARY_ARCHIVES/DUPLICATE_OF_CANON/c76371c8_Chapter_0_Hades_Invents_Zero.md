# Chapter 0
## The Negotiated Void

*Scritch.*

It was the sound of a Null Chisel hitting the Axiomatic Floor.

The sound was not loud—barely a whisper of steel against stone—but it was **Hard**. It was the sound of a mistake being carved into the foundation of the universe.

In the basement of the cosmos, where the Wi-Fi was nonexistent and the geometry was damp, Hades wiped stone dust from his hands. He was not a god yet. He was just the Stonemason. He wore a grey apron that smelled of wet slate and finality.

"It doesn't fit," he said.

Zeus, who was currently operating as the Chief Draftsman, looked up from the table. He was holding the **Compass**—the Divine Instrument of Enclosure. He looked tired. His beard was full of eraser shavings.

"What doesn't fit?" Zeus asked. "The circle? I drew it perfectly."

"The circle is fine," Hades said. "It's the stuff inside it. You're trying to put 'Everything' into a container labeled 'Something.' You have leftovers."

He held up a bucket. It was filled with squirming, vibrating refuse—the shavings of reality that hadn't made the cut. Irrational numbers. Angles that summed to 181 degrees. The color of a sound that shouldn't exist.

"These are the Remainders," Hades said. "The scraps from your perfect geometry. Where do you want me to put them?"

Zeus waved a hand dismissively. "Just... sweep them under the rug. Ignore them."

"I can't," Hades said. "The rug is a plane. The floor is a plane. If I put these between them, the floor becomes uneven. The table legs will wobble."

Zeus frowned. He hated wobbling. He was the God of the Steady Hand, the Lord of Vector Stability. A wobbling table was an insult to his function.

"Fine," Zeus snapped. "What do you propose?"

Hades reached into his pocket and pulled out a small, circular stone. It looked ordinary, except for the fact that looking at the center of it made your eyes slide off.

"I made a hole," Hades said.

Zeus squinted. "A hole?"

"A designated absence. A place where we agree to put the things that don't fit." Hades placed the stone on the pristine, white table. "I call it **Zero**."

Zeus poked it with the sharp end of his compass. The tip passed through the stone and scratched the table.

*Scritch.*

"It's a defect," Zeus said. "It's a crack in the Hardness. If we let 'Nothing' into the system, the 'Somethings' will get ideas. They’ll start wondering where they go when they stop being Ratios."

"On the contrary," Hades said, his voice flat and heavy, like a tombstone settling into soft earth. "If you name the Nothing, you can count the Something much faster. You can say there are *Zero* Krakens in the pantry. Currently, you have to list every single thing that *is* in the pantry just to prove a Kraken isn't one of them. It’s a massive waste of clock-cycles."

Zeus paused. He looked at the mountain of paperwork on his desk—the infinite lists of "Things That Are."

"Efficiency," Zeus muttered. He liked efficiency almost as much as he liked symmetry.

"And," Hades added, leaning in, "it solves your Remainder problem. The Zero is a basement. I will take all the scraps—the irrationals, the monsters, the things that scream when you measure them—and I will store them in the Zero. Out of sight. Out of mind."

Zeus looked at the bucket of vibrating chaos. Then he looked at the quiet, empty stone.

"And what do you want for this... service?" Zeus asked.

"The rights to the interest," Hades whispered.

"Interest?"

"The Zero is a mortgage," Hades explained. "I lend you the space to build your Integers. But space costs. Every time a human counts to ten, they use my Zero to hold the place. Everyday they balance a ledger, they use my Zero to clear the debt. I want the accumulated value of that usage."

Zeus laughed. It was a rhythmic, integer-based laugh. He checked the math. Zero times anything is Zero. The Stonemason was asking for nothing.

"You want the product of the void?" Zeus grinned. "Done. Take the trash. Take the interest on nothing. Give me the stabilizer."

Hades handed over the Null Chisel and the Void Stone.

"Deal," Hades said.

He turned to leave. He didn't smile. Stonemasons don't smile; they only bevel edges.

He knew what Zeus didn't. He knew that counting starts at Zero, not One. He knew that by accepting the Zero, Zeus had unknowingly agreed to let the Infinite back into the Square.

He created a Backdoor.

As Hades descended the stairs to the basement, he heard the sound of Zeus working above him—the frantic, rhythmic scratching of the Compass, drawing circles around the Zero, trying to contain the leak he had just authorized.

*Scritch. Scritch. Scritch.*

The sound of a mortgage being signed in stone.

The universe was now Hard. It had edges. It had a floor.

And in the center of the floor, perfectly hidden, was a hole that went all the way down.
