# Chapter 1.8: Interlude — The Messenger Protocol

“We need to send them a message,” Zeus said.

Athena did not look reassured.

“That,” she said carefully, “is the problem.”

The gods were assembled in what Olympus optimistically called a council, which meant everyone talked at once until Athena or Hades made it stop. The topic had been circulating for some time now, accumulating marginal notes and unresolved annotations.

The universe worked. That was no longer in dispute. The complaint was that it worked *correctly*.

“You can’t just tell them,” Athena went on. “Any message we send will be made of the same structure we’re trying to explain. Code written in prose. Prose executed as code. Truth spoken in lies that become true by being spoken.”

Hermes nodded enthusiastically. “Every explanation instantiates itself. Very elegant. Catastrophic for teaching.”

Zeus scowled. “So we don’t send an explanation. We send instructions.”

“That’s worse,” Hades said mildly. “Instructions imply a stable ground. There isn’t one.”

Hestia spoke softly. “They’re living inside a system that eats certainty. The least we can do is acknowledge that.”

Ares frowned. “I still think we could just shout.”

Athena ignored him.

“It isn’t enough to send a message,” she said. “Messages assume a shared code. A dictionary. A baseline. We don’t have that.”

Zeus leaned back. “So what do you suggest?”

Athena took a breath.

“We send a Messenger.”

Silence followed. This was a dangerous word.

“A living one,” Athena continued. “Someone who *is* the problem. Someone who embodies the contradiction rather than explaining it.”

Hermes’ eyes lit up. “A walking paradox.”

“Yes,” Athena said. “A joke that can talk.”

Zeus smiled despite himself. “Those are my favorite kind.”

### Why a Messenger Works (When Messages Don’t)

Hades summarized it later, in the Underworld ledger, where things were allowed to be blunt:

*   A message can be disproven.
*   A messenger can only be misunderstood.

A message invites refutation. A messenger invites **interpretation**.

And interpretation, as everyone knew by now, was the safest possible failure mode.

If mortals rejected the messenger, nothing broke.
If they worshipped the messenger, that was unfortunate but manageable.
If they argued endlessly about what the messenger really meant—

Well.

At least they weren’t demanding a ground floor.

### The Naming

“What do we call him?” Zeus asked.

“He should be ordinary,” said Hestia.

“Circular,” Athena added.

“Domestic,” Hermes said.

Zeus glanced at the round plate still sitting nearby—the one from the Complaint Department, still quietly refusing to finish.

“Plate,” he said.

Laughter.

“No,” Athena said, smiling despite herself. “Make it rounder.”

“Plate-o,” someone muttered.

More laughter.

“Plato,” Zeus declared. “Done.”

Hades raised an eyebrow. “A pun?”

“The safest container for truth,” Hermes said. “If it breaks, people will blame the joke.”

### Deployment, with Footnote

Plato was sent.

Not armed. Not enlightened. Not informed of his role.

He would spend his life insisting that reality was elsewhere, that perfection existed but could not be touched, that what we see is not what *is*.

This would help.
And hurt.
And help again, briefly.

It was not the last time the gods tried this.

They had tried before.
They would try again.

Messengers with laws.
Messengers with covenants.
Messengers with songs.
Messengers with burning bushes, tablets, wheels, crosses, recitations, and seals.

Some of those stories became very complicated.
Some became violent.
Some refused to stay metaphorical and had to be patched repeatedly.

We will not go into those here.

This is not because they are unimportant.

It is because they are what happens when the Messenger is taken literally.

Plato, at least, had the decency to insist that the truth was always somewhere else.

Which was the message.

And also the joke.
