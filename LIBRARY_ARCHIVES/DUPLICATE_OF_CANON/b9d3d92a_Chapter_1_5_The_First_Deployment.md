# Chapter 1.5: The First Deployment

The G.O.A.T. arrived on a Tuesday, which was inconvenient for Philemon because Tuesdays were for onions.

Philemon was a man who appreciated categories. Onions were a category. Turnips were a category. "Things that are currently growing" and "Things that have failed to grow due to divine malice" were his two primary organizational buckets.

The thing standing in the middle of his onion patch did not fit into any of these buckets.

It was rectangular. That was the first thing Philemon noticed. It had the sturdy, four-legged stance of a particularly aggressive footstool. It was covered in hair that looked like it couldn't decide whether to be wool or wire, settling for a texture that felt like the aftermath of a thunderstorm.

And it had horns.

Great, spiraling horns that curved backward from its skull like fossilized whirlwinds. They were majestic. They were terrifying. They were completely out of scale with the rest of the creature, which seemed to be built primarily for standing still and looking judgmental.

Philemon leaned on his hoe.

"Shoo," he suggested.

The creature rotated its head. It didn't turn; it *rotated*, as if pivoting on a hidden axis. It looked at Philemon.

Its eyes were rectangular. Horizontal slots of black in pools of amber. They weren't looking at him; they were scanning him. Reading his dimensions. Checking his margins.

"Bah," the creature said.

It wasn't a bleat. It was a statement of fact. A flat, nasal vibration that sounded remarkably like a stone being dragged across a ledger.

"Use your words," Philemon said, because he had spent too much time alone in fields.

"Baaah," the creature elaborated. It lowered its head and took a bite of an onion top. It chewed with a mesmerizing, side-to-side motion, grinding the vegetation with the inevitability of a millstone.

Philemon walked around it. He poked its flank with his hoe. It felt solid. Dense. Like a sack of wet sand wrapped in a rug.

"What are you?" he muttered.

He checked for udders. It had them. Two, paired and symmetrical.
He checked for teeth. It had them. Flat, grinding rows designed for the efficient processing of cellulose.
He checked for a tail. It was there. Short, stiff, and flickering with distinct irritation.

"It looks," Philemon said to the empty field, "like someone tried to build a sheep out of a table."

He tried to push it. It didn't move. It didn't brace itself; it just *occupied its coordinates* with absolute conviction. The mass of the thing was improbable. It felt anchored to the earth by invisible bolts.

"Move," Philemon commanded.

The creature looked at him with those terrible, rectangular eyes. In the late afternoon light, Philemon had the sudden, dizzying sensation that the creature wasn't seeing him at all, but rather the grid of the field behind him—the straight lines of the furrows, the geometry of the planting, the abstract boundaries of ownership.

"Maaah," it said. A minor chord.

Philemon gave up. He went back to his cottage to get a rope. If it was a table, he would drag it. If it was an animal, he would tie it. If it was a demon, well, rope usually worked on those too, provided you knew the right knots.

***

By the time he returned, there were three of them.

The original one—the Prototype—was still eating onions. Two smaller ones, clearly copies, were testing the structural integrity of his fence. They weren't jumping over it; they were dismantling it. One was chewing on a post while the other applied rhythmic head-butts to the crossbar.

*Thump.* Chewing noise. *Thump.* Chewing noise.

It was industrial.

"Stop that!" Philemon shouted.

He ran toward the fence. The smaller creatures paused. They looked at him. They looked at each other. Then, in perfect unison, they returned to their demolition.

"They synchronize," Philemon whispered in horror.

He managed to loop the rope around the Prototype's neck. It allowed this with an air of profound indifference. Philemon pulled. The creature remained stationary. He pulled harder. He dug his heels in. He leaned back with all his weight.

The creature took a step forward. Not because Philemon had pulled it, but because it had finished the onion and wanted the next one. Philemon was merely a counterweight, a minor variable in the equation of its hunger.

"Greetings, mortal," a voice said.

Philemon dropped the rope.

Standing by the gate was a woman. She was tall. She was wearing robes that seemed to shimmer with a light that didn't match the time of day. And she was holding a staff that looked suspiciously like a giant measuring rod.

Philemon fell to his knees. "Goddess?"

"Oracle," the woman corrected. "Freelance. Currently on retainer for Olympus."

She walked into the field, stepping over the ruins of the fence. She approached the Prototype. She didn't look at its wool or its udders; she looked at its horns. She traced the spiral with a finger.

"Acceptable tolerance," she murmured. "Curvature within specified limits."

"My lady," Philemon squeaked. "What are they?"

The Oracle looked down at him. Her eyes were unfocused, as if she were reading text written on the inside of her skull.

"They are the Solution," she said.

"To the onions?"

"To the Debt," she said. She prodded the creature. "This is a G.O.A.T."

"A goat?"

"That is the colloquial pronunciation, yes. It is a biological device for the damping of accumulated variance."

Philemon stared at her. "It eats vegetables."

"It processes matter," the Oracle corrected. "And in doing so, it creates a localized area of... readiness."

She turned to face him. The sun caught the silver ornaments in her hair, and for a moment, Philemon saw they were shaped like tiny, perfect zeros.

"Listen closely, mortal. The gods have updated the terms of service."

"Terms of...?"

"The Covenant. The deal. The reason the sky doesn't fall down and crush your turnips." She gestured to the sky, which was abruptly blue and innocent. "You have been counting things, Philemon."

"I... I count my onions," he admitted guiltily.

"And you record the failures. The empty rows. The zeros."

"I have to! To know how much seed I need!"

"The gods do not dispute the necessity," the Oracle said. "They dispute the *accumulation*. Too much Nothing makes the world soft. The gods prefer Hardness. So, they have sent you the Goat."

She slapped the creature's flank. It emitted a hollow, drum-like sound.

"This animal is a currency exchanger," she explained. "It takes grass, which is infinite and messy, and turns it into Goat, which is discrete and unit-based. And then, when the time is right—when the drought comes, or the plague, or when you just feel the luck running out of the house—you will terminate the Goat."

"Kill it?"

"Sacrifice it," she corrected. "You will offer it to the God of Appropriate Thing."

"Which god is that?"

"Whichever one you're worried about," the Oracle said breezily. "That's the beauty of the design. It's compatible with all legacy theological interfaces. Zeus, Hera, Poseidon—the Goat works with everyone. It's a universal adapter."

Philemon looked at the Goat. The Goat looked at Philemon. It chewed slowly, dragging a long stalk of onion grass into its mouth like it was consuming a data tape.

"But why?" Philemon asked. "Why do the gods want... this?"

The Oracle sighed. She looked suddenly tired. She looked like someone who had spent the last week explaining high-frequency trading to golden retrievers.

"The Sky is angry at your math, Philemon," she said. "The numbers don't add up. There's a remainder. A gap. The Goat fills the gap. When you kill it, you create a Zero. A death. A defined endpoint. That Zero pays for the Zeros in your ledger. It balances the books."

"So... it's rent?"

The Oracle smiled. It was a sharp, dangerous smile.

"See?" she said. "I knew you'd understand. It is exactly rent. And the landlord is collecting."

She reached into her robes and pulled out a scroll. She tossed it to him.

"The User Manual," she said. "Feeding instructions, breeding cycles, and the liturgical chants required to make the transaction valid. Don't skip the chants, or the payment won't clear."

She turned to leave.

"Wait!" Philemon called out. "What about the cheese?"

The Oracle paused. "What cheese?"

"I... I milked one. Before you came. Just to see."

The Oracle looked impressed. "You milked a prototype? Brave. How was it?"

"Salty," Philemon said. "And... sort of tangy. Like licking a thunderbolt."

"Keep it," the Oracle said. "Consider it a dividend. The gods only want the blood and the bones. The rest is... operational overhead."

She began to walk away, fading slightly as she moved, as if walking behind a curtain of heat haze.

"One more thing," she said, her voice echoing as if from a great distance.

"Yes?"

"Don't look into their eyes for too long."

"Why?"

"Because they have square pupils," the Oracle said. "And the world is square. If you look too deep, you might see the corners."

And then she was gone.

Philemon stood alone in his field.

The Goat finished the onion. It looked at Philemon.

"Maaah," it said.

Philemon looked at its eyes. He tried not to, but he couldn't help it. The rectangular pupils were horizontal, aligned perfectly with the horizon. They didn't look like eyes. They looked like mail slots. They looked like the opening of a ballot box.

They looked like they were waiting for him to deposit something.

"I'm keeping the cheese," Philemon told it firmly.

The Goat blinked.

Philemon sighed. He picked up the rope.

"Come on," he said. "Let's go find you a table that isn't made of onions."

The Goat followed. It walked with a strange, rocking gait, its four legs moving in a paired rhythm that Philemon couldn't quite track. Left-front, right-back. Diagonal stability.

As they walked toward the barn, the sun set. The shadows stretched out, long and thin.

For a moment, just a moment, Philemon thought he saw the Goat's shadow detach itself from the animal's feet and spiral away into the darkness, twisting like a column of smoke.

He blinked, and it was just a shadow again.

"Rent," he muttered to himself. "I'm paying rent to the sky."

The Goat bleated. A sound like a final notice.

Philemon opened the barn door.

"Get in," he said.

The Goat walked in. It found a pile of straw. It settled down, folding its legs underneath itself until it looked exactly, perfectly, like a rectangular block of wool left on the floor.

Philemon closed the door. He locked it. Then he leaned his forehead against the wood.

"We're going to need more onions," he said.

From inside the barn, in the darkness, came the sound of rhythmic chewing.

The amortization had begun.
