# THE HANDPRINT
## Book 5: The Transfinite Horizon — Final Chapter

The river was lower than it had been in the morning.

Veth stood at the edge where the water met the clay bank and watched the level drop by the width of a finger while she watched. Not drought. Just the river doing what rivers did — finding its own level, adjusting to something upstream that nobody here could see yet.

She had been watching rivers long enough to know this was not a warning. It was information.

Behind her, the forty-three were doing what the forty-three always did when they stopped moving long enough to breathe: building. She could hear the rhythm of it. Stone on stone. The particular sound that meant someone was checking an angle with their body rather than a tool — that slight pause, the shift of weight, the moment of listening that preceded the decision to keep going.

Kaelen had been gone for eleven days.

He had not said he was leaving. He had simply not been there one morning, and his absence had the quality of a thing that had been prepared for carefully and in advance, the way the river prepares for its own flooding by laying down silt in particular places for months before the water rises.

She understood why. She had understood for longer than she'd admitted.

The moment he stayed, he became the standard.

She knelt at the river's edge.

The clay here was the colour of the inside of her wrist. Not brown — something between brown and gold and the particular grey of early light. It was the colour of the world before it decided what it was going to be.

She pressed her right hand into it.

Not hard. Not with the deliberateness of someone making a record. Just — contact. Palm first, then fingers settling into the give of it, feeling the cold, feeling the way the clay accepted the pressure without resistance and without complete yielding, the way it held the shape of her hand while making clear the shape was temporary.

She held it there.

Felt the river moving three inches from her knuckles. Felt the mud's slow breath — the way it was still draining somewhere deep, still connected to the water table, still in conversation with the whole system she couldn't see.

She thought about the cave.

Kaelen had described it once, the caves in the desert hills where the old paintings were. The hands pressed in ochre, in charcoal, in the residue of a fire that had been cold for ten thousand years. He had stood in front of them, he said, and felt something that wasn't quite grief and wasn't quite recognition and wasn't quite either.

*Like meeting someone at the end of a very long journey*, he had said, *who had left a letter for you at the beginning of it. And the letter just said: I knew you were coming.*

She thought about the person who had made the first one. Who had stood at a cave wall with a mouthful of ochre and a hand pressed to the stone and understood — without the words for understanding it — that the hand was the message.

Not: *I was powerful.*
Not: *I was here.*

Just: *this is what a hand looks like.* This is the shape of the thing that built and measured and held and let go. This is the instrument. This is the fifth of the arm. This is the manus, the little man, the tool that introduced the spiral into the hexagon.

This is what reached.

The river made a sound she didn't have a name for. Not quite a word. More like the thing a word is made of before someone decides what it means.

She thought: *the water was real.*

She thought: *we swam in it.*

She thought: Kaelen is three days northeast, tasting beer in some city none of us have seen yet, reading the flow rate in the colour of the water in his cup, deciding in thirty seconds whether the Archon has found the recipe yet. Deciding whether to stay long enough to say anything. Probably not. Probably gone before they know he was there, leaving only the question hanging in the air: *why does the Bir taste different this season?* The question that is the seed. The question that is the wobble the inspector can't shim.

He doesn't need to answer it. The question is enough. The question is the hiss, recognised.

She lifted her hand.

The print remained. Five fingers, a palm, the faint line where her wrist met the clay. Already the edges were softening slightly where the water was wicking up through the bank. By morning it would be smoothed. By the next rain it would be gone.

This did not seem to her like loss.

It seemed to her like the correct behaviour of a handprint. Like the thing doing exactly what it was supposed to do, at exactly the right speed, for exactly the right duration.

Long enough to be seen. Short enough to not become a relic.

She stood up.

Behind her, the sound of the building had changed. Someone had found the angle they were looking for — she could tell by the silence that followed the checking, and then the resumption of the rhythm, slightly different now, slightly more certain. Straight enough. Keep moving.

The river was lower again by a finger's width.

She watched it for a moment longer — this ordinary, continuous, completely indifferent process of the water finding its level — and felt something she recognised from the moment in the corridor when she had first blinked and seen the hollow bricks for what they were.

Not revelation. Revelation was too large a word, too evaluated, too shimmed.

Just: *oh. Yes. This.*

The river moving.

The mud holding and releasing.

The handprint already becoming something the water would eventually reclaim and carry downstream and deposit somewhere she would never see, mixed into the silt of the delta, the new soil of the mouth, the foundation of whatever came next.

She turned back to the work.

The river was real.

That was enough.

That was everything.
