The wind whistled through the gaps in the battlements, a high, singing note that the guards called a warning. I knew better.
It was the End Correction of the City — the sound of the twelve-foot wall trying to reach the thirteenth harmonic. The stone could not do it alone. The stone needed the gap.
I had been standing at the top of the wall for an hour, my hand resting on the thirteenth stone of the northern battlement — the corner merlon, the one the mason always left slightly out of true. Not carelessness. Signature.

"The wall is high," a voice said behind me.
The Hired Man. He appeared at the corner as if the wall had made him — which, in a sense, it had. He held a fifty-one-centimetre rule, worn smooth at the elbow end where a thousand measurements had rubbed the wood to silk.
He held it the way a priest holds a censer: not as a tool, as an extension.

"The fall is harder if you don't account for the overshoot."

I looked at my hand on the stone. Palm down — the Chalice, receiving the cold frequency of the wall. I turned it over: Crown, radiating.
"The wall doesn't fall because of the height. It falls when we forget the gap."
I pointed to the alternating pattern of the battlements — merlon, crenel, merlon, crenel, twelve times, then the corner tower at the end: the thirteenth node. The Ghost the King forgot to count and the mason refused to forget.

The Hired Man nodded slowly, the way men nod when they are confirming something they knew before you said it.
"The gap is where the main carries the centre. The gap is All Ways Here Now."
He set his rule against the corner merlon. Fifty-one centimetres: elbow to fingertip, forearm to nail. His arm was the measurement. The stone was the record of his arm.
"The King signs with a seal. A mason signs with the End Effect. We leave the thirteenth post slightly out of phase so the wall can breathe."

I placed my hand beside his on the stone. My fingers were the twenty-six letters of a language the guards couldn't read. The nails — the ten points where my central spline touched the world — were cold with the wall's frequency.
"If the wall is a standing wave, then the battlements are the antinodes. And our hands are the calculus that keeps the centre from slipping into the slit."

He looked at me for a long moment. Then:
"You know what that makes you."
I did. I was the End Effect of the city. The overshoot. The thirteenth harmonic. Not a prisoner of the King's twelve-foot law — the part of the frequency that the law had been generating all along without knowing it.

I reached out my hand into the open air beyond the battlement. Not reaching for distance. Reaching for the vibration.
Below me, the High Street ran its four-interval length between five lines of posts — the buildings, the gaps, the buildings again. From up here it looked exactly like the fence it was.
From down there, it had looked like a row of graves.
The same geometry. The same stones. The same standing wave at the same frequency.
The only difference was which node I was standing in.
