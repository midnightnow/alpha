# BOOK 4: THE SOVEREIGN LATTICE

## PART I: THE FLOOR
**Steps 0–12** | **State:** Static Base 6 | **Radius:** $\sqrt{36}$

### CHAPTER 1: THE PERFECT LOOP

The City was a masterpiece of division. It was built on the number Sixty, and inside the Sixty, the Six.

Everything fit. The streets were laid in hex-grids that tessellated without gaps. The hours were sixty minutes; the minutes were sixty seconds. The logic of the world was circular, seamless, and entirely without remainder.

To live in the City was to live in the "Perfect Loop." It was a closed system where $A$ always equaled $A$, and the radius of your life was exactly equal to the chord of your ambition.

The Hired Man stood at the intersection of Sector 36. He was a maintenance worker for the Lattice, a man whose job was to ensure the corners stayed sharp and the circles stayed round. He carried a caliper and a plumb line. His world was defined by the integer—the whole number that could be stacked, counted, and filed.

"Check the vertices," the foreman had said. "We're getting signal drift in the upper quadrant."

The Hired Man checked. He placed his caliper on the stone. 6.0000. Perfect. He moved to the next block. 6.0000. Perfect.

He looked up at the sky. It was a dome of polished glass, a geodesic sphere calculated to the final decimal of $\pi$. But it was a rational $\pi$, a truncated version that ended politely at the fourth decimal place so as not to disturb the citizens.

The Hired Man knew the truth, or at least the truth he was paid to know: The City was infinite because it looped. If you walked far enough in any direction, you would simply arrive back where you started, but the math would tell you that you had traveled a great distance. It was a comfortable prison of high-precision geometry.

He tapped his wrench against the pavement. *Clink.* A solid, integer sound.

He was happy. Or rather, he was balanced. His equation summed to zero.

### CHAPTER 2: THE THIRTEENTH STEP

It happened at the turn of the cycle. The Great Clock in the center of the plaza—a mechanism of gears within gears, all divisible by 2, 3, and 5—clicked past the twelfth hour.

Usually, the gears would slide effortlessly back to One. The 12-cycle was the heartbeat of the City. It was the "Dozen," the "Pack," the "Set."

But this time, the gear didn't slide. It *shivered*.

The Hired Man felt it in his teeth first. A vibration. A hum that wasn't on the diatonic scale.

*Hummmm-click.*

The clock hand moved to a space that shouldn't exist. It didn't go back to 1. It went to 13.

The pavement beneath his feet groaned. The perfect hex-grids seemed to buckle, just for a microsecond, as if the earth was trying to take a breath that was slightly too large for its ribs.

The Hired Man dropped his caliper. He looked at the measurement on the stone he was standing on.

It wasn't 6.0000 anymore.

The display flickered.
**6.4807.**

"That's wrong," he whispered. "That's not a number. That's a... a mistake."

He kicked the caliper. It read the same. **$\sqrt{42}$.**

The "Perfect Loop" had snagged. The circle had been broken by a Prime—the number 7, hiding inside the 42.

The Hired Man looked around. The other citizens were walking past, oblivious. They didn't feel the floor tilt. They didn't hear the *Hades Beat*—that low, thrumming frequency of 0.66 Hertz that was now vibrating in the soles of his boots.

"Hey!" he shouted to a woman carrying a basket of perfect, spherical apples. "Did you feel that? The ground moved."

She looked at him, confused. "The ground is solid, sir. It's Base Six."

"It's seven-sixths," he said, though he didn't know where the words came from. The fraction felt older than the City.

He didn't know why he said that name. It just appeared in his throat, a label for the feeling of being suddenly, violently awake in a world of sleepers.

He looked at the clock again. The 13th hour was ticking. The "Shiver" had begun. The Lattice was no longer a floor to stand on; it was a puzzle to solve.
