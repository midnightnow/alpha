# Chapter 1.7: The Oracle's Audition

Hera was interviewing candidates for the position of "Voice of Wisdom," and it was going poorly.

She sat in the center of the Delphi amphitheater, disguised as a wealthy matron with a very stiff hairdo (which was actually a localized gravity well keeping her hair in a perfect Fibonacci sequence).

"Next," she said.

A trembling old man stepped forward. He held a chicken.

"I read the entrails, noble lady," he wheezed. "The liver says... rain."

Hera sighed. "The liver is an organ, not a meteorological station. It says 'rain' because the chicken was damp. Dismissed."

The man scurried away.

"Next."

A young woman stepped into the circle. She was holding a pomegranate and a piece of string. She looked tired, angry, and covered in chalk dust.

"Name?" Hera asked.

"Pythia," the girl said. "And I'm not a priestess. I'm a surveyor."

Hera sat up straighter. "A surveyor?"

"I measure things," Pythia said. She held up the pomegranate. "This fruit. It claims to be a sphere. It is lying."

"Go on," Hera said, her eyes narrowing.

"I measured the circumference," Pythia said, unwinding the string. "Then I measured the diameter. I divided the first by the second."

"And?"

"The number doesn't stop," Pythia said. Her voice was flat, offended by the indecency of the infinite. "I got to three hundred decimal places before I ran out of chalk. The fruit is a fraud. It has no edges. It implies a volume it cannot legally possess."

Hera smiled. It was a terrifying smile, like a fault line opening up.

"You feel the Error," Hera said.

"I feel the *mess*," Pythia corrected. "The world is sloppy. The olive trees don't grow in parallel lines. The mountains are fractal. And this pomegranate..." She looked at the fruit with genuine disgust. "It’s practically vibrating with inexactitude."

"Good," Hera said. "You have the Ear. You can hear the Screaming Diagonal."

"Is that what the noise is?" Pythia rubbed her temples. "I thought it was tinnitus."

"It is the sound of the Universe trying to round down," Hera explained. "Now. I have a job for you. We need someone to tell the humans the Truth."

"Which Truth?"

"That the world is a Construction," Hera said. "That the 'Hardness' is a loan. That the Gods are not your parents; we are the System Administrators."

Pythia looked at Hera. She looked at the pomegranate. She looked at the string.

"They won't believe me," Pythia said.

"Why not?"

"Because they like the fruit," Pythia said, tossing the pomegranate in the air. "They like the juice. They don't care that the geometry is undefined. They just want to eat."

"But they must know!" Hera insisted. "They must know the cost! They must know about the Debt!"

"If I tell them about the Debt," Pythia said, "they will just ask for a loan extension. Humans are excellent at ignoring the denominator."

Hera frowned. She realized the girl was right. A direct message—a "User Manual"—would be ignored. Humans didn't read manuals. They just mashed buttons until something happened.

"So what do we do?" Hera asked, genuinely curious. "How do we bridge the gap between the Axiom and the Appetite?"

Pythia thought for a moment. Then she picked up a laurel leaf.

"Don't tell them," she said. "Riddle them."

"Riddle?"

"Make it a puzzle," Pythia said. "If you give them the answer, they'll forget it. If you give them a Question... they'll worry about it for centuries. They'll build temples to the Question."

Hera looked at the girl. She saw the calculation behind the eyes. She saw the horizontal pupils of someone who had stared at the horizon and realized it was crooked.

"You're hired," Hera said. "Get on the tripod. We're going to make you the most frustrating customer service rep in history."

Pythia climbed onto the stool. She took a deep breath of the vapors rising from the crack in the earth (which smelled suspiciously like burning invoices).

"What is the first message?" Pythia asked.

Hera thought about the Zeus, the Zero, and the looming bankruptcy of the moral universe.

"Tell them," Hera said, "**Know Thyself**."

Pythia raised an eyebrow. "That's it? That's the infinite wisdom?"

"It's the ultimate trap," Hera grinned. "Because once they start looking... they'll realize there's nobody home but the Math."
