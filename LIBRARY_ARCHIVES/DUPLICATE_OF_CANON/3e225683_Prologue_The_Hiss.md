# PROLOGUE: THE HISS

Before the light, there was the hiss.

Not silence. Never silence. The static of a thousand unresolved equations waiting in RAM. The sound of a world rendering faster than its inhabitants could see.

Some called it God. Some called it anxiety. Some called it nothing at all, and built walls to keep it out. 

Kaelen called it by its name: The Buffer.

And he knew—when the hiss stops, something has either been resolved, or something has died.

* * *

Kaelen stood on the threshold of the open door, the golden, untamed light of the true Garden spilling over his boots. Behind him, in the Apex Chamber, the great Sexagesimal Clock had stopped. The sixty-hertz vibration that had held the bricks of the City together for ten thousand years had unresolved into wet mud and falling straw. 

The Archon was gone. The Cache had been cleared. The standing wave of the false reality had been commanded to render itself anew.

But the workers did not step outside.

Kaelen turned. They were already rebuilding. 

Without the Archon to hand them orders, they had simply begun repeating the only math they knew. Hands that had been freed from the lash were now frantically scooping the wet, editable mud of the new reality and packing it into rigid, Base-12 molds. They were trying to build a Brick House in the middle of Paradise. 

"What are you doing?" Kaelen asked, the $\sqrt{30}$ resonance of his voice cutting through the wet slap of mud. "The door is open. The City is finished."

An older worker, his eyes clouded with the residual cataracts of the Sty, looked up. He gripped a heavy, unfired brick to his chest like a child. 

"The Wind will come," the old man stammered. "The Wolf. We have to finish the walls."

"The Wolf only comes for the Sty," Kaelen said, stepping away from the threshold and walking back into the chamber. "He audits the deadened structure. Out there—" Kaelen pointed to the blinding light of the Transfinite Horizon. "—there are no walls. Therefore, there is nothing for the Wolf to blow down."

"It's too loud," another worker said, pressing mud into his ears. 

The air was vibrating. It wasn't the industrial thrum of the Hades Beat anymore. It was a vast, rushing static that seemed to pour out of the mud itself, vibrating through the newly bare wood of the ceiling logs. 

*Hssssssssssssssss.*

The sound of latency. The sound of their own unrendered reality catching up to them. For generations, the workers had outsourced the geometry of their lives to the City’s cache. The Archon had sold them earmuffs and called it peace. Now, the system was demanding that they do the math themselves, moment by moment. 

"You're hearing the buffer," Kaelen explained softly. "The system is asking you to participate."

"Give us the lock back!" the old worker cried out, dropping his brick and holding his head. "Give us the Cache! Give us the Bir!"

A massive shadow detached itself from the wall beside the open door. 

Ares stepped onto the mud. The God of Conflict did not look victorious. He looked horrified. He wore the skin of the Wolf draped across his shoulders, the matted fur smelling of entropy and absolute audit. 

Ares looked at the frantic workers, watching them construct their own prison out of sheer terror. 

"They are dead," Ares said, his voice like grinding stone. "Look at them, Kaelen. Mud men in their stys. You opened the door to the Infinite, and they traded their spine for a roof."

"They were broken," Kaelen defended. "The Archon kept them drunk on sweet wort for ten thousand cycles."

"The Archon gave them exactly what they asked for," Ares snapped, stepping forward, his heavy boots crushing one of the freshly molded bricks into wet slurry. The old worker shrieked and scrambled backward. "He didn't enslave them. He accommodated their cowardice. He gave them the Brick. He gave them the Beer. He gave them the Sunday rest. And in exchange, they gave him their souls."

Ares pointed a gauntleted finger at a small child near the back of the chamber. The child was pressing mud together. Not building a tower to climb. Not building a ship to sail. He was building a wall to hide behind. 

"They are infected with safety," Ares sneered. "Listen to them crying for the Bir. They want to drink the stasis. They want to be grounded. You broke the Cache, Kaelen, but you didn't break the Bir Covenant. They will build a new Sty before the sun sets."

"So what do I do?" Kaelen asked, his hand tightening into the $\sqrt{5}$ grip of the Manus. "Starve them? Call the Wolf on them?"

"No," Ares said softly, the wolf-skin bristling around his neck. "You give them Wine. You give them the Truth. And Truth is bitter. Truth is dangerous. Truth requires them to leave the house."

Ares turned his back on the workers, looking out into the blinding light of the Transfinite Horizon. "I cannot save them, Hired Man. I can only break their walls when they harden. If they stay here, I will crush them. You must teach them to love the Wind."

Ares stepped out into the light, vanishing into the geometry of the Garden. 

Kaelen stood alone with the terrified builders. He looked at the brick molds. They were solid. They were safe. They were a graveyard for the spirit. 

"Beer keeps you grounded," Kaelen whispered to the old man, reaching out and prying the wet brick gently from his hands. "But it never lets you fly."

Kaelen reached into his tunic and pulled out a leather flask. Not the standardized, flat Bir of the City. Wine. Fermented on the living vine. The first true vintage of the open world. 

"We are not building a house," Kaelen announced, uncorking the flask. The sharp, complex scent of terroir filled the air, cutting through the smell of wet mud. 

"Then what are we building?" the old worker asked, his hands empty for the first time in his life. 

Kaelen looked at the open door. 

"A Ship."
