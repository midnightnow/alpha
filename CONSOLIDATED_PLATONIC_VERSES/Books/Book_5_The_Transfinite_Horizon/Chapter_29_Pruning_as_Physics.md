# BOOK 5: THE TRANSFINITE HORIZON
## CHAPTER 29: PRUNING AS PHYSICS

The first shoots of the Ash-Vine had overtaken the scrap-iron of the old Reef-Leader's platform. They were a brilliant, aggressive green, but as they climbed, the bottom-most leaves began to turn a brittle, translucent brown.

"It's dying!" the Leader shouted, his voice cracking. "The Silt is winning! Look, the growth is failing at the base!"

He reached for a bucket of water, but Veth stepped in his path. 

"Don't water the past," she said firmly. "The browning you see isn't death. It's the **Decay Rate ($\phi \approx 0.618$)** in action. It is the most vital part of the physics."

She reached out and pinched off one of the translucent leaves. It didn't crumble; it dissolved into a faint mist of grey data that drifted down to the mud.

"The vine only has a finite amount of **Processing Power**," Veth explained to the gathered exiles. "If it tried to maintain every leaf it ever grew, it would eventually run out of Render. It would become static. It would become a monument to what it *used* to be."

She pointed to the tip of the vine, which was surging upward with incredible speed.

"By letting the base decay, the vine releases the **Spun Sun** it spent yesterday. It 'un-observes' its old form so it can focus entirely on the **Now**. The browning leaf is the fuel for the green vector."

The exiles watched as a group of brown leaves fell simultaneously. In response, a massive new leaf unfurled at the top of the mast, its surface area doubling in seconds. 

"This is the **Pruning Protocol**," Veth said. "In the City, you were rewarded for holding on—for accumulating chits, for saving memories, for building walls. In the Garden, you are rewarded for **Letting Go**. The more you can prune from your identity, the more energy you have to climb."

The boy who had planted the first coordinate marker looked at his own hands. He realized he was still holding a piece of rusted chain from his old wreck. He looked at the vine, then at the chain. 

He dropped the chain into the mud. 

Immediately, the Hiss in his ears dropped by another three decibels. The world became slightly sharper. He didn't feel "poorer" for losing the iron; he felt **faster**.

"Physics doesn't care about your attachments," Veth whispered to him. "It only cares about the **Flow**. Prune the dead weight, and you will find that the air is a ladder."
