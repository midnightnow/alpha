# BOOK 5: THE TRANSFINITE HORIZON
## CHAPTER 14: THE NOMINAL TAX

The Priest did not use a sword to stop the **Ship of Bir**. He used a **Word**.

"You are carrying **Anarchy** in that hold," the Priest called from the pier, his voice perfectly calibrated to the **Base-60** frequency of the city. He didn't look at the crew, but at the ledger in his hands, as if the physical presence of the boat was merely a secondary effect of the data it generated.

Tal (He Brews) paused his wooden paddle. The smell of the fermenting salt-Bir was thick around him—sharp, living, and unapologetically unrefined. "It’s just beer, Priest. Grain, water, and time. There's no law against the sun and the wait."

"By naming it 'Beer,' you attempt to bypass the **Register**," the Priest replied, his silver caliper catching the sun in a clinical, sharp flash. "You use a common word to hide a systemic debt. But I name it **Systemic Contagion**. And the moment I name it so, the maths of this Garden change. You are no longer 'Neighbors'; you are **Pathogens**. Your 'Warped' crew is no longer ballast; they are **Structural Failures**. You owe the City the **Nominal Tax** for the resolution of this error."

Kaelen stepped to the rail, his own **Manus (5)** gripping the coarse, salt-dusted wood of the railing. "Your names are just **Hard Supports** for a deferred debt, Priest. You name the 'Hiss' as 'Sin' so you can sell the earmuffs and the blinders. You name the 'Wobble' as 'Disease' so you can charge for the cure."

"I set the **Standard**," the Priest countered, his eyes finally meeting Kaelen's. They were eyes that saw the world in grids and percentages, incapable of rendering the shimmer of the Open Render. "Without my names, the Wolf is at your door. Without the register, there is no order, only the Hiss."

"The Wolf is already here," Kaelen said, pointing to the Priest's shining caliper. "He's the **Silt** in your register. You’ve named the world into a **Sty**, and now you’re drowning in the labels. You can't tax the wind, and you can't eval the sea."

Kaelen turned to the Sealer. "Launch. The Priest's words don't have enough **Mass** to stop the tide."

The Sealer leaned into the tiller, his twisted shoulder absorbing the torque of the sudden current. The ship wrenched itself away from the pier, leaving the Priest standing on the vitrified edge of the City, his silver tool useless against the irrational roar of the water.
