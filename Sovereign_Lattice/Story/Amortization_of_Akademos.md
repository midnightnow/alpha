# The Amortization of Akademos (The Debugging of Kallipolis)

**Location:** Akademos Minor (The Island of Failed Theorems)  
**System Status:** Critical Tension Accumulation  
**User:** Plato (Admin Level: Philosopher-King)  

"Eh eh eh."

"Eh eh eh."

Plato woke up slowly, confused, head spinning slightly. The noise hurt, like nails over a blackboard. Keratin against stone.

**Eh Eh Eh.**

More insistent now. The sound wasn't coming from outside. It was coming from the corner of the room, specifically from the coordinate where the wall met the floor—a right angle that was currently vibrating at a frequency that made his teeth itch.

**EH EH EH.**

He carefully opened his eyes and saw something staring back at him.

**Meh Meh Meh.**

It stood in the shadow of his perfect cube-shaped bedframe. It was small, sturdy, and radiated a profound, stubborn refusal to be impressed by anything. It looked like a sheep, but the wool was too coarse, too much like static electricity trapped in fiber. Its horns spiraled in recursive helices, clockwise and counter-clockwise, locking into the air itself. Its pupils were horizontal rectangles, scanning the horizon of the room rather than the depth.

The sound was almost like an evil laugh in his haze. Perhaps that wine last night was a little too good. Or perhaps the ambrosia had been cut with too much Void.

**The experimentation must continue.**

**MEH EH EH EH.**

"Move it. Time to get up," Plato whispered to himself, though the command felt directed at the beast.

He swung his legs out of bed. The floor tiles—cut to exact integer proportions—hummed under his bare feet. The creature did not flee. It simply bleated again, a flat, nasal dissonance that cut through the silence of the divine geometry.

"What on earth was this thing?" he puzzled, rubbing the sleep from his eyes. "Like a sheep, but useless for wool. Useless for milk. You smell of... ozone and wet clay."

The goat tapped a hoof on the stone. *Click. Click.* It was counting.

Plato froze. He remembered the blueprints from Olympus. The meeting in the Fabrication Workshop. Hera sculpting the chassis. Prometheus designing the horns as variable inductors. Hades insisting on the horizontal pupils to monitor the boundary.

"A G.O.A.T.," Plato murmured. "**G**eometric **O**ffset **A**mortization **T**ool."

The creature blinked. A slow, deliberate shutter closure.

"You're not here for the pasture," Plato realized, standing up. He walked to the window. Outside, his perfect garden was dying. The olive trees, arranged in a perfect dodecagon, were turning gray. Their leaves were becoming equations instead of foliage. The water in the fountain had stopped flowing, frozen in a perfect parabolic arc because the ratio of gravity to pressure had resolved too cleanly.

"There's no leak," Plato said, panic rising in his chest. "I eliminated the leak. I squared the circle."

**MEH.**

The sound was sharp. Accusatory.

"No," Plato corrected himself. "I eliminated the remainder. And now the system has nowhere to put the error."

He looked back at the goat. It was chewing on the corner of his rug. Not eating the fabric—eating the fringe. The loose threads where the weave ended.

"You're hungry," Plato said. "But not for grass."

He walked to his desk. The scrolls were piled high. *The Republic*. *The Timaeus*. Drafts of laws that assumed humans were rational integers. He picked up a stylus. His hand shook. The **Short Cord** problem was manifesting physically. The distance from his hand to the inkwell felt variable, expanding and contracting like a breathing lung.

"The Priest says we have to eat in sync with the Whir," the goat seemed to say, though it only opened its mouth. "He says if we don't, the Zeros will leak into our bones."

Plato laughed, a dry, cracking sound. "The Priest. Yes. They worry about the math. They worry about the **Tensegrity Constant**."

He looked at the goat. He looked at the perfect, dead garden outside. He looked at his own hands, made of ratios.

"I built a cage of triangles to keep out the madness," Plato whispered to the beast. "But I discovered that the madness is the math."

The goat stopped chewing. It stared at him with those ancient, cynical eyes. It knew what came next. It knew the protocol. **Square + Circle = ERROR.** **Square + Circle - (Goat) = STABLE.**

"The experimentation must continue," Plato said again, but his voice was weaker now. He knew what the continuation required. It wasn't more geometry. It wasn't more laws.

He reached out and touched the spiral horn. It was warm. Vibrating with the stored charge of a thousand unresolved diagonals.

"You're not useless," Plato said softly. "You're the buffer. You're the **Lunch Truce**."

The goat bleated. **Meh.**

It wasn't a laugh. It was a receipt.

Plato walked to the door. He needed to find a knife. Not to kill the beast—not yet—but to cut the tension. To introduce the **Snag**. To let the water flow again.

"Wait here," Plato said. "I need to find something imperfect. Something that doesn't fit."

The goat lay down on the perfect square of the floor. It closed its horizontal eyes. It began to chew the air, digesting the silence, waiting for the Sacrifice that would allow the sun to rise without shattering the sky.

Outside, the diagonal screamed in the walls. The Zeros pooled in the corners. But in the room, for one long, golden moment, the cord was exactly long enough.

Plato stepped out into the hallway. He did not calculate the latency. He just walked.
