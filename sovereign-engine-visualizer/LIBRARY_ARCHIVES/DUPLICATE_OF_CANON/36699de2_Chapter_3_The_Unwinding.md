# THE UNWINDING
## Book 5: The Transfinite Horizon — Chapter 3

The man was sitting on a half-built wall, holding his right wrist in his left hand as if it were something that had been given to him and he wasn't sure he wanted it.

Kaelen had been watching for several minutes before he approached. Not because the man was hiding — he was in plain view, on the low stone wall that the forty-three were building along the northern edge of the clearing. He was visible. He was present. He was, in every measurable way, participating.

But his right hand was shaking.

Not the tremor of cold. The tremor of a mechanism that has been run in one direction for so long that it has forgotten how to stop. The vibration of a gear still spinning after the machine has been turned off.

"What did you do?" Kaelen asked.

The man looked up. His name was Adrik. He had been one of the last to walk through the door. Not from courage — from the simple fact that his hands had stopped working on the line three days before the Refresh, and the foreman had moved him to counting rather than opening. Counting was lighter work. Counting kept him alive long enough for Kaelen to break the wall.

"In the city?" Adrik said.

"In the city."

"I opened."

Kaelen waited.

"Jars," Adrik said. "The preserving house. Twelve-base grid, six rows of jars, sixty jars per row. Counter-clockwise, quarter-turn, pull. Counter-clockwise, quarter-turn, pull. Eight hours." He looked at his wrist. "Twelve years."

"What happened to the jars after you opened them?"

"I don't know. Someone else took them. Someone else filled them. Someone else closed them." He flexed his fingers, wincing. "I only opened."

Kaelen knelt beside the wall Adrik had been building. It was uneven — not the honest unevenness of a dry stone wall where each stone keys its wobble to the next, but the brittle unevenness of a man whose right hand can no longer grip with the precision his eye demands. The wall wanted to be straight. The hand could not make it so.

"Show me," Kaelen said.

Adrik demonstrated. His left hand gripped an invisible jar. His right hand turned — counter-clockwise, quarter-turn, a motion so practiced it was almost elegant. Smooth. Efficient. A mechanism perfected over twelve years and three hundred thousand jars.

Then Kaelen said: "Now close it."

Adrik stared at him.

"Close the jar."

Adrik's right hand turned clockwise. Or tried to. The motion was clumsy, uncertain, as if his wrist contained a ratchet that only clicked in one direction. His face contracted — not from pain exactly, but from the strangeness of asking a joint to reverse what it had been told for twelve years was its only purpose.

"It won't—" he started.

"I know," Kaelen said. "Try again. Slowly."

The clockwise turn was ugly. A quarter of the arc that the counter-clockwise turn covered. The fingers fumbled. The wrist caught at angles that the opening motion had worn smooth.

"You're wound up," Kaelen said. "Twelve years of torque in one direction. Every quarter-turn you made stored rotational debt in the joint. The body is designed to balance — wind against unwind, push against pull, the blade against the bowl. You were only ever the blade."

Kaelen picked up a stone. Heavy, rough, the size of two fists. He set it in Adrik's lap.

"Lift it."

Adrik lifted it. A different motion — vertical, through the shoulder, the whole arm engaged. His wrist, unloaded from the rotational axis it had been tortured on, relaxed visibly. The tremor quieted.

"Now set it down."

He set it down.

"Now dig." Kaelen handed him a flat stone, edged like a trowel. "Here, at the base of the wall. We need to see what the ground is doing."

Adrik dug. A different motion again — extension, the forearm reaching forward, the wrist in a neutral plane, the fingers gripping something that resisted differently than glass. The earth gave way under the stone edge with a sound like a held breath releasing.

"Now lift the stone again. Set it on the wall."

Adrik lifted. Set. The stone found a place in the course that wasn't perfect — Kaelen could see the gap — but was honest. The weight bore down through the wall's own wobble and found equilibrium.

"Now rest."

Adrik stopped. He looked at his right hand. The tremor had not vanished — twelve years of torque does not discharge in twelve minutes — but it had changed. From the sharp, metallic vibration of a gear spinning free to something slower. Deeper. More like a pulse than a rattle.

"In the city," Kaelen said, "you were a gear. One gear on one axis, spinning in one direction, connected to a machine so large you couldn't see the edges of it. And when the gear wore out—"

"They sent me to the doctor," Adrik said quietly. "He said it was arthritis." He pronounced the word carefully, the way you pronounce a word from a language you don't speak. A word someone gave you. A diagnosis borrowed from another register.

"Did he tell you what arthritis means?"

Adrik shook his head.

"It means your joints hurt. In Greek. He said the same thing you told him, in a language you can't read, and charged you for the translation."

A brief silence. The sound of the others building, further along the wall. Stone on stone. The pause that meant someone was checking an angle with their body.

"What did he give you?" Kaelen asked.

"A paste. For the pain. And a brace." Adrik touched his wrist where the brace used to be. "So I could go back to the line."

"He insulated the Hiss," Kaelen said. "He wrapped your emergency signal in padding so you couldn't hear it anymore. Not because he was cruel — because the system needed you opening jars, and the system paid him to keep you opening jars, and neither of you were allowed to ask the question that would have ended both your jobs."

"What question?"

"Why does a man open jars for twelve years and never close one?"

Adrik looked at the wall he was building. At the stone he had just set. At his hands — the right one still trembling, but differently now, with the memory of digging and lifting and setting as well as opening. With the first faint impression of a counter-rotation.

"Here," Kaelen said, "you are not a gear. You are a wheel. A wheel touches the ground at every point. It pushes and pulls. It winds and unwinds. It goes forward and it can go back. A gear only meshes with the gear next to it. A wheel touches the earth."

He stood up.

"Build the wall. Then dig the foundation for the next course. Then carry water from the river. Then sit and eat with the others. Different motions, different loads, different axes. The torque discharges across the whole body instead of accumulating in one joint."

He paused.

"And on the seventh day, do none of it. Lift no stone. Dig no hole. Open no jar and close no jar. Let the spring unwind completely. Touch the mud with your bare hands and remember that your body is not a mechanism. It is a process. Like the river. Like the story. Like everything that's still alive."

Adrik nodded slowly. He picked up another stone, weighed it in both hands — both hands, the wounded and the whole, sharing the load for perhaps the first time in twelve years — and set it on the wall.

It wobbled slightly.

He moved on to the next one.

Straight enough.
