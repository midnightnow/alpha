# The Platonic Verses

**"The towers didn't fall. They liquidated."**

In the Golden Age of Atlantis, mathematicians weren't just engineers—they were **Geometric Alchemists**. They discovered that if you layered enough promise-notes for future goats into complex theorems, you could build towers that defied gravity simply because the debt was too heavy to lift.

It was the perfect system. Until Poseidon called the margin.

Now, the world has suffered a massive **Axiomatic Deleveraging**. The "Hardness" of reality has been revoked. The survivors are hiding in huts made of mud and sticks, afraid to draw a straight line lest they incur a high-interest loan from the gods.

Enter **Plato**, a retired risk-manager hiding on a desolate island, eating figs to stay solid. He knows the truth: the Bronze Age Collapse wasn't an invasion. It was a **Liquidity Crisis of the Soul**.

As a new generation starts building ships that float on speculation rather than buoyancy, Plato must teach a young traveler the most dangerous lesson of all:

**You can't eat a receipt, and you can't stand on a postulate.**

*The Big Short* meets *The Odyssey* in this tragicomic exploration of why the universe always demands its shavings back.
