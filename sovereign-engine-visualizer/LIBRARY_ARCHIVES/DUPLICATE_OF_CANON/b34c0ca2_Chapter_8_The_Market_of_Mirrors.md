# THE MARKET OF MIRRORS
## Book 5: The Transfinite Horizon — Chapter 8: The Merchant's Mirror

Kaelen walked through the newly established market at the edge of the Garden. It was a strange, brittle place. The forty-three were already trading — not seeds, but artefacts they had salvaged from the City ruins.

A woman was holding a piece of vitrified silk, stained with river mud. She was arguing with a man who had travelled from the southern deltas, a nomad with mud-caked boots and a necklace of raw, unpolished stones.

"Look at him," the woman hissed as Kaelen approached, gesturing to the nomad. "He brings nothing but rocks and dirt. He smells of the Wild. How can we build a civilisation with people who refuse to even wash the soil from their hands?"

Kaelen looked at the woman. She was wearing a tattered, "fine" City robe. Her face was clean, but her eyes were weeping. The white lesion of the Sty was beginning to cloud her vision, making her squint in the afternoon sun.

"He carries his dirt on his boots," Kaelen said. "You carry yours in your eye."

The woman stiffened. "I am a citizen of the Lattice. I know what is fine and what is foul."

"You are living in a field, clutching a piece of dead silk, and calling it wealth," Kaelen said. "The nomad brings stones — raw material for the hearth. He brings the Wild — the external audit you need to stay honest. But you can't see the stones because you're fixated on the obsessions of a man who doesn't use a Base-12 ruler."

Kaelen stepped between them. He picked up one of the nomad's raw stones. It was heavy, honest, and cool.

"The Archon's greatest trick wasn't the walls," Kaelen told the woman. "It was the Market. He gave you fine things so you would feel superior to the Wolf. He gave you a language of 'dirty foreigners' so you would never have to look at the Sty you were building around your own soul."

He handed the stone to the woman.

"Wash your eyes, Elara. The nomad isn't the threat. The fine goods are. They are the bandages that keep the infection warm."

---

The woman looked at the stone for a long time.

It was heavy in a way the silk was not. The silk had weight — barely — the accumulated ghost of a process she had never seen, performed by hands she had never touched, in a city that was probably already silt. The silk was a receipt for someone else's labour, carried across a desert by someone else's feet, valued by a system that no longer existed.

The stone was just itself. Grey, roughly oval, cold from the river, with a fracture plane along one side that caught the light. It was what it was. It did not require a market to establish its value. It did not require a merchant to translate its worth. It sat in her hand and communicated directly: I am hard. I am heavy. I will bear your weight or break your toe. There is no negotiation.

She looked at the nomad. He was watching her with the patience of someone who had done this before — stood in the gap between two economies and waited for the local one to notice that his stones were more honest than their silk.

"Where did you find them?" she asked.

"The river," he said. "Three days north. The bank where the current slows and the heavy things settle."

She turned the stone over. On the underside, a faint impression — a shell, fossilised, pressed into the rock by a weight of time she couldn't calculate. Something had lived in this stone. Not recently. Not in any human timeframe. In the timeframe of the river itself.

"It's old," she said.

"Everything is old," the nomad said. "The silk is old too. It just pretends to be new."

She set the silk down on the trading blanket. It collapsed into itself, formless without a hand to hold it, shapeless without a body to drape. The stone sat where she placed it and did not move.

Kaelen watched from the edge of the market. He didn't intervene again. The intervention had been the stone. The rest was between the woman and the weight.

Some days later, he noticed the silk was still on the blanket. Nobody had taken it. The stone was in the foundation of Elara's section of the wall, third course, north face, bearing weight.

She had chosen the honest thing.

Not everyone would.
