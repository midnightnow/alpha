# THE SCENE: STEALING THE HEX-FIRE

*From the Heroine's Narrative: The Pandora Hinge*

The Boy was stacking blocks in the dust of the High Street. He was the King’s son, or might as well have been, building a perfect right angle against the noon sun. 

"Three blocks up," the Boy said, tapping the vertical post. "Four blocks across. And five connecting them. The Mason's Triangle. It’s perfect. It never falls."

"It’s a cage," the Girl said. She was squatting beside him, running her thumbnail—the 5th post of her hand—through the loose silt of the street. "You only built it because you want the things inside to stop moving."

The Boy frowned. "It’s the Law. Three, four, five. The post, the ground, the roof. What else is there?"

"The Mountain," she whispered. 

"Mountains aren’t math. Mountains are just dirt. The King’s Grid is the only math."

The Girl stood up. The sun was passing its zenith, the shadows beginning to stretch. "Your math is dead," she said. "You think the 5 is the roof. But the 5 isn’t the end. The 5 is the door."

He laughed, a sharp, Flatland sound. "Show me."

The Girl didn't touch his blocks. If she touched the King's 3-4-5 grid, she would be playing by his rules. Instead, she stepped directly into the path of the sun. 

"Look at the Shadow," she commanded.

The Boy looked. The vertical post of his blocks—3 units high—was casting a shadow. But the Girl placed her hand on top of the structure, her fingernail extending exactly one unit past the edge. She didn't add a block; she added a *remainder*. She added the "Nail."

"You have 4 units on the ground," she said. "But what if I make the ground 12?" 

She walked 12 paces away from the blocks, tracing a long line in the silt with her toe. It was the Roman Road. The long rail of the King's ambition.

"Now," she said from the end of her 12-unit line. "Connect my feet to the top of your post. Not the 3. Connect it to the 5." She held up her hand, spreading her five fingers. "The Queen's post."

The Boy squinted. He traced the invisible hypotenuse in the air, from her feet (the end of the 12) to her hand (the top of the 5). 

He did the math in his head. The King's son always knew the math. 
$5 \times 5 = 25$. 
$12 \times 12 = 144$. 
$25 + 144 = 169$.

The root of 169 was 13.

The Boy gasped. He fell backward into the dust. "Thirteen..." he whispered, looking at the invisible diagonal connecting them. "There's no 13. The King says the wall stops at 12."

"The wall stops at 12," the Girl agreed, her eyes catching the 60° angle of the fading sun. "But the bridge crosses at 13. The 13 is the Agley path. The one that breaks your square."

She had opened the Pandora Hinge. By using her own *unresolved remainder*—her physical reach, her nail, her shadow—she had converted the Boy's rigid 3-4-5 cage into the sweeping 5-12-13 architecture of the High Street. 

"The 5 isn't the roof that holds you down," she said, letting her hand drop, the invisible 13 collapsing back into the silt, waiting to be summoned again. "The 5 is the Queen. The Diagonal. She's the only reason your walls don't fall over when the world spins."

The Boy looked at his 3-4-5 blocks. They didn't look like a castle anymore. They looked like a box. A grave.

He looked up at the Girl. "How did you do that? How did you steal the extra one?"

"I didn't steal it," she smiled, a terrible, beautiful geometry in her expression. "It's always been there. You were just too busy counting the posts to notice the gaps."
