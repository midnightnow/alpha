# Chapter 1: The Complaint Department

The Complaint Department of Olympus was located in a building that shouldn't have existed but did anyway, wedged between the Temple of Eternal Geometry and a small café that served only prime-numbered pastries.

The building itself was a compromise—Hera had insisted on symmetry, Apollo demanded a golden ratio, and Hephaestus had built it on a Tuesday when he was hungover and couldn't be bothered with anyone's aesthetic requirements. The result was a structure that looked like someone had tried to divide a pentagon by a circle and given up halfway through.

Behind the marble counter sat Metis, Goddess of Practical Wisdom and the only deity willing to staff the department. She had accepted the position after Zeus swallowed her during a particularly heated argument about whether infinity was a *number* or just *very rude*.

She had, through sheer determination and what she insisted was "strategic gastric persistence," managed to continue working from inside Zeus's frontal lobe. The Olympians had eventually agreed it was easier to just give her the job than to explain why the King of Gods had a fully furnished office in his sinuses.

The door opened.

A human walked in.

This was already suspicious. Humans didn't *usually* make it to Olympus unless they were heroes (arrogant), demigods (complicated), or lunch (unfortunate). This human was carrying a ceramic plate and wearing the expression of someone who had been personally victimized by geometry.

"I'd like to file a complaint," the human said.

Metis looked up from her scroll, which was currently computing the theological implications of parallel parking. "Name and grievance?"

"Archimedes. And my grievance is with *π*."

Metis set down her stylus. This was going to be one of *those* complaints.

"Go on."

Archimedes placed the plate on the counter. It was round. Aggressively round. The kind of round that made you uncomfortable about how definitive circles were supposed to be.

"I measured this plate," Archimedes said. "The distance across is exactly one cubit. The distance *around* is three-point-one-four-one-five-nine-two-six-five-three-five-eight-nine-seven-nine—"

"We get it," Metis interrupted.

"—and it *keeps going*," Archimedes continued, his voice rising. "I've been writing for six hours. I've filled fourteen scrolls. My apprentice quit. My wife thinks I've joined a cult. And the number is *still happening*."

He slammed his hand on the counter. The plate wobbled but remained perfectly, infinitely circular.

"I want to know *where the number is coming from*," Archimedes demanded. "This is a *plate*. It's made of *clay*. Clay is finite. Clay *ends*. So why doesn't the number end?"

Metis pinched the bridge of her nose. She'd been expecting this ever since Hades brought that damn Zero upstairs.

"Have you tried," she said carefully, "just calling it *three*?"

Archimedes stared at her like she'd suggested he measure the ocean with a teaspoon.

"It's not *three*. It's three-point-one-four-one-five-nine—"

"Yes, we've established you have excellent penmanship," Metis said. "But functionally, for the purposes of plate-ownership, does it *matter* that the number doesn't stop?"

"*Yes*," Archimedes hissed. "Because if the number doesn't stop, that means the circle isn't *finished*. Which means this plate isn't actually *round*. Which means I've been eating off an *approximation*."

Metis could feel Zeus stirring somewhere in the back of her conceptual real estate. This was exactly the kind of pedantic crisis that made him start smiting people.

"Look," she said, "the plate is round *enough*—"

"*Enough?*" Archimedes looked like he might vibrate out of his tunic. "The Olympians are supposed to be in charge of *Forms*! Of *Perfection*! And you're telling me that the perfect circle has a *measurement* that never *ends*?"

"Technically," Metis said, flipping through a very old ledger that smelled like cave-dust and regret, "the circle is perfect. The *number* is just... *verbose*."

"That's not how math works!"

"It is now," Metis said. She found the entry she was looking for. "As of approximately six months ago, per Amendment Null-Point-Zero, ratified by Hades and Zeus during a contract negotiation that I was *not* invited to."

She turned the ledger around so Archimedes could read it.

**AMENDMENT Ø: ON THE INTRODUCTION OF ZERO AND ASSOCIATED REMAINDERS**

*Whereas the Olympians require a method to denote absence,*
*And whereas Hades has agreed to warehouse all irrational byproducts,*
*It is hereby agreed that certain measurements may continue indefinitely,*
*Provided they are assigned to the Underworld for long-term storage.*

Archimedes read it twice. Then a third time, because the legalese made his eyes water.

"So you're telling me," he said slowly, "that the reason π doesn't end is because *Hades is hoarding the decimals*?"

"In a basement, yes," Metis confirmed. "Along with √2, φ, *e*, and several numbers we don't let people name because they start screaming if you write them down."

Archimedes looked at his plate. Then at Metis. Then back at his plate.

"Can I... can I talk to him?"

Metis sighed. She reached under the counter and pulled out a small bell that looked like it had been carved from a single piece of obsidian.

"Ring this," she said. "But I warn you: Hades doesn't do refunds."
