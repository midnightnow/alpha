# BOOK 5: THE TRANSFINITE HORIZON
## CHAPTER 8: THE DIAGNOSIS

The clinic smelled of antiseptic and old paper. It was a clean smell, but underneath it lay the sour note of stagnant water—the smell of a system that no longer flushed.

Kaelen sat on the examination table. The paper crinkled beneath him, a thin barrier between his body and the sterile white room. Across the desk sat the Specialist. Her eyes were covered by a thin band of polished glass—a Visual Cache, mandated for all Senior Evaluators to prevent "Perceptual Fatigue."

She didn't look at him. She looked at the readout on her screen, which displayed the biometric data streaming from the sensors in the room.

"Your render rate is inconsistent," she said. Her voice was flat, calibrated. "You are processing reality at approximately 60 frames per second. Continuous active geometry. Do you know how dangerous that is?"

Kaelen looked at his hands. They were steady. "It's called seeing."

"It's called Hyper-Rendering Disorder," she corrected. She tapped a key. A new graph appeared, spiking red. "Most citizens operate at 12 frames. They download the Consensus Cache. They see what is agreed upon. You are trying to solve the geometry of every leaf, every shadow, every breath. You are burning out your optic tract."

"I'm not burning out," Kaelen said. "I'm waking up."

The Specialist sighed. She reached for a prescription pad—a physical chit, still used for serious diagnoses. "You are experiencing symptoms of Dissociative Reality Syndrome. You perceive wobble where there is structure. You perceive debt where there is value. You perceive the Wolf where there is safety."

"The Wolf is at the door," Kaelen said. "The vaults are hissing."

The Specialist's hand paused. For a microsecond, the glass over her eyes flickered. A glitch in the Cache. "The vaults are secure. The hiss is ambient noise. It is the sound of civilization functioning."

"It's the sound of pressure," Kaelen said. "The Grievance is accumulating. You cut the grain, you cut the sheep, and now you're cutting the people who notice the blood."

The Specialist stood up. She walked around the desk. For the first time, she lifted the glass band.

Her eyes were red-rimmed, the pupils dilated unevenly. The Sty lesion was there—a white film over the left pupil, thick as cured glue. She was blind to the Prime. She saw only the Grid.

"I am trying to help you," she said softly. "I can prescribe a Visual Cache. Stronger filtration. You won't see the wobble. You won't see the cracks. You will sleep without dreaming. You will be safe."

"And who pays for the prescription?" Kaelen asked.

"The Council subsidizes safety," she said.

"No," Kaelen said. "I pay. I pay with my sight. I pay with my hands. I pay with the truth I can no longer speak."

He stood up. The sensors in the room shrieked—a high-pitched alarm as his biological resonance spiked. He was rendering the room faster than the sensors could track. To the machine, he was blurring.

"You can't diagnose me," Kaelen said. "I'm not ill. I'm just not cached."

He walked to the door. The Specialist didn't stop him. She couldn't. Her authority relied on the patient accepting the diagnosis. If he refused the label, the treatment was void.

"Come back when the hiss becomes a scream," she said to his back. "When you can no longer bear the weight of the real. We will be here. We have always been here."

Kaelen stepped into the corridor. The hiss was louder here. It wasn't ambient noise. It was the sound of the walls straining against the pressure of the Grievance stored inside them.

He walked past the waiting room. Dozens of people sat in rows, wearing their Visual Caches, staring at blank screens, downloading the Consensus. They looked peaceful. They looked dead.

One of them looked up. A young man. He had lifted his cache slightly. His eyes were wide, terrified, and clear.

"Does it stop?" the young man whispered. "The seeing?"

Kaelen paused. "No. But it gets quieter. When you stop fighting the wobble."

"I want to fight it," the young man said. "I want to see the cracks."

"Then take off the glass," Kaelen said. "And be ready to run."

The young man lifted the cache. He blinked. He winced as the light hit his unfiltered eyes. He looked at the wall. He saw the hairline fracture running through the plaster. He saw the damp spot where the pipe had leaked. He saw the reality behind the paint.

He smiled. It was a painful smile. "It's ugly," he said.

"Yes," Kaelen said. "It's real."

Kaelen walked out into the street. The sun was setting. The shadows were long. He rendered every one of them. He felt the fatigue in his bones—the cost of the Prime. But he also felt the flow.

Behind him, in the clinic, the Specialist put her glass back on. She sat down. She opened a new file. *Patient 44: Refusal of Care. Prognosis: Terminal Reality.*

She filed it under **E**. For Eval.
