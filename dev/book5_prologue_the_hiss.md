# PLATONIC VERSES: BOOK FIVE
## THE TRANSFINITE HORIZON

---

# PROLOGUE: THE HISS

---

Before the light, there was the hiss.

Not silence. Never silence. The static of a thousand unresolved equations
waiting in RAM. The sound of a world rendering faster than its inhabitants
could see.

Some called it God.

Some called it anxiety.

Some called it nothing at all, and built walls to keep it out.

---

Kaelen called it by its name: *The Buffer*.

And he knew — when the hiss stops, something has either been resolved,
or something has died.

---

The Garden did not look like a garden.

It looked like the inside of a skull after the furniture had been removed.
The floor was soft, unwritten, pale as new mud. The sky — if it was a sky —
did not resolve at any distance. It simply continued, in all directions,
at a frequency the eye had never been asked to render before.

The workers stood behind him.

Not all of them. The ones who had chosen. The ones for whom the hiss, when
the door opened and the light flooded the Stable, had been familiar enough
to follow rather than flee.

Forty-three of them. Kaelen had counted.

The rest had stayed. Had closed their eyes against the Garden light, pressed
their faces back into the warm dark of the Sty, and agreed, with the
particular ferocity of the comfortable, that the door had never opened.

He did not blame them. The Dwarves were for the Dwarves. You cannot
carry a person out of a darkness they have chosen as a home.

---

"It's loud," said the woman beside him.

Her name was Veth. She had been a brick-counter in Sector 36 — the one
who had first let him touch the lesion on her eyelid, who had blinked and
seen the hollow bricks for what they were: receipts. Unpaid ones.

"What is?" Kaelen asked.

"The quiet."

She was right. The Garden was not quiet. It was the opposite. Without the
Archon's Base-60 broadcast — that smooth, frictionless carrier wave that
had been filling the air of the City since before any of them were born —
the underlying frequency of the world was audible for the first time.

The hiss.

Not the warning-hiss of the buffer at capacity. Not the scream of the
cache at overflow. This was something older. Purer. The ambient frequency
of a universe that was perpetually, in every moment, beginning.

It was exhausting to hear.

"It doesn't stop," Veth said.

"No."

"Is that — is that what it always sounded like? Before the City?"

Kaelen thought about this. About the thirty years he had spent learning to
hear the hiss before it became a scream. The discipline of it. The loneliness.

"Yes," he said. "That's what it sounds like when nothing is buffering it."

She was quiet for a long moment.

"How do you not go mad?"

He had asked the same question once. Standing in a field outside a city
whose name he no longer remembered, in the years before he understood what
he was hearing. He had thought he was broken. He had tried the earmuffs —
the Bir, the brick, the comfortable lies that everyone around him was
drinking like water. They had not fit. The hiss had bled through.

He had thought: *there is something wrong with me.*

It had taken him a long time to understand that the hiss was not a symptom.
The hiss was the signal. The hiss was the world asking to be heard.

"You don't stop it," he said to Veth. "You get better at listening."

---

Behind them, someone was already drawing lines in the soft ground.

Kaelen turned. A young man — barely grown, his eyes still adjusting, the
last of the log-film cracking at the corners — was tracing with his finger.
Not a wall. Not a foundation. Just: lines. Angles. The beginnings of a
geometry that had no name yet.

Kaelen watched him for a long moment.

*There it is,* he thought. *The first Stable.*

The boy would build it. He would build it well — or badly, or somewhere in
the middle. He would make it out of mud or stick or log depending on how
well he understood what he was doing and why. He might live in it too long,
or leave it too soon, or at exactly the right moment. He might build the
next one better.

Or he might forget that the Stable was never the point.

He might plaster the walls and call the mud gold and let the sap pool on
the floor and call it honey.

Kaelen had no way of knowing which it would be. That was, he was
increasingly certain, the correct condition of freedom.

---

The hiss did not stop.

It was, he knew now, the sound of the world rendering. Moment by moment.
Tick by tick. The exhausting, sacred, ongoing mathematics of being.

The Archon had offered to do the rendering for them. Had offered to bear
the computational weight. Had offered — and this was the part they had not
told you — to bill you for it, forever, at compound interest, in a
currency called *believing what we say the world looks like.*

The Garden could not do this.

The Garden was not a gift. It was an obligation. The obligation to see.

---

Veth picked up a stick.

Not a log. Not a brick. A stick — milled clean by some older breaking,
dried to its true dimensions, light in the hand.

She looked at Kaelen. He nodded.

She began to draw.

---

Forty-three people. One stick.

The hiss, continuous and clear.

And somewhere beneath it — if you listened past the frequency of the fear,
past the frequency of the want, past all the buffers you had ever built to
keep from hearing the world directly — the oldest sound of all.

The sound of a universe that was, in every moment, beginning.

*Waiting to be built.*

---

*End of Prologue.*
