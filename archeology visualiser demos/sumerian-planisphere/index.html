Curator's Note

This circular tablet divides the sky into 8 sectors, each containing constellations and star markers. The Babylonians used such planispheres for astronomical calculation, ritual timing, and interpreting celestial omens.

📅 The Dating Debate

The tablet: K8538 was physically created c. 700 BCE during the Neo-Assyrian period, found in King Ashurbanipal's library at Nineveh.

The controversy: Some researchers (Bond & Hempsell, 2008) claim the observations recorded date to ~3123 BCE, based on matching star positions using precession calculations. This "5,500 years old" claim is disputed by mainstream Assyriologists.

Most scholars view it as a standard Neo-Assyrian astronomical text, possibly copying earlier Babylonian sources from ~1200-700 BCE.

𒀭 The Science of MUL.APIN
"The Babylonians developed systematic astronomical observation centuries before the Greeks."

— Dr. Wayne Horowitz, Hebrew University

The planisphere K8538 is part of a rich tradition of Mesopotamian astronomy codified in texts like MUL.APIN ("The Plough Star"). These scribes tracked heliacal risings, lunar cycles, and planetary motions with remarkable precision using their base-60 (sexagesimal) number system.

The 8-sector division represents equal 45° arcs of the celestial sphere, with each sector spanning approximately 45 days of the solar year. Star positions were measured in cubits (KÙŠ) and fingers (ŠU.SI) from reference stars.
