Being a True & Profitable Instructionary Tale

The Parable of Ember & Umber

for Aspiring Penmen of Every Station

✦

In the long-ago of the scriptorium, when letters were still learning their shapes, there were born of a single pomegranate two brothers: Ember, and Umber.

Ember came first and made himself known immediately. He was the flesh of the fruit — red, swelling, bright as a coal, full of juice. He caught every eye that entered the room. The novices pointed. The abbot nodded. Here, they said, is a thing of substance. And Ember agreed with them, heartily.

Umber arrived after, and was almost thrown away.

He was the seed — small, dark, hard, easily lost between the flagstones. The colour of old earth, of spent charcoal. He made no noise. He offered no juice. He sat quietly in the palm of the one brother-monk who happened to notice him, and said nothing at all in his defence.

◆
II. Of Their Natures

Ember grew fat on admiration. In the days that followed, the novices squeezed him and his juice ran down their wrists — bright, sweet, abundant. They wrote with it. The letters came out red and vivid and caught the sun magnificently. The abbot framed them. Visitors praised them.

But by the fortnight's end the letters had faded. The juice had dried to nothing, leaving only a faint sticky ghost upon the vellum. Where had the meaning gone? The novices scratched their heads. They squeezed Ember again. He gave what he had, which was the same as before: surface, shine, and the promise of a thing rather than the thing itself.

Umber, meanwhile, had been pressed into a small pot of dark earth by the quiet monk, and left on the sill. Within a week he had split his hull and sent up a pale green thread. Within a month he was a plant. Within a year he had given ten thousand seeds.

✦
III. The Lesson of the Pen — Which Is the Same Lesson Told Again

Now hear me, Penman, for this is where the parable becomes your craft.

You hold the broad-edged nib as a sower holds the seed. And in that nib there live two brothers still, inseparable, though utterly unlike.

The downstroke is Umber. It falls heavy, broad, dark, direct — the full width of the nib laid flat against the page. It is the seed pressed into the earth. It does not perform. It simply is. Bold. Present. The whole weight of the hand behind it.

The hairline is Ember. It is the thinnest thread of the nib's corner, light as a rumour, bright as a spark. It connects. It radiates. It is necessary — but it is not the substance. It carries the eye from weight to weight, as a vine carries a fruit.

Between these two — the dark weight and the bright thread — there runs a third thing: the arc. The diagonal stroke, found only when the nib is held at its true angle, neither flat nor upright but across. This is the hypotenuse. This is the radian. This is the stroke that connects the downstroke's foot to the hairline's head, and in doing so completes the square and reveals the letter's true proportion.

The arc is not dark like Umber. It is not bright like Ember. It partakes of both, and by that union it carries meaning — the letter made legible, the angle justified, the geometry confessed.

◆
IV. The Error of the Novice

The novice, seeing Ember's brightness, makes all his strokes as hairlines. The page shimmers. It catches the light magnificently. But when you stand back, there is nothing to read — only a field of radiating threads going nowhere, anchored to nothing, meaning less the more you look.

Another novice, hearing that the downstroke is the substance, bears down with all his force on every stroke alike. The page is dark and heavy and honest, but it too is unreadable — a row of identical posts with no vine between them, no arc to show the eye how the weights relate.

Both have mistaken the part for the whole.

The true penman knows that the bold downstroke is the seed — small in count, immense in gravity — and the hairline is the juice, necessary for life but not the life itself. You make few downstrokes. You make them with your whole arm, your whole intent, your whole faith in the mark. The hairlines come freely, almost without effort, for they cost nothing and weigh nothing. And then — then — with the nib at its corner angle, you draw the arc that squares the circle and earns the letter's right to stand on the page.

✦
V. The Moral, as Simply as May Be Said

Do not mistake the shine for the substance. A poppy seed could not be more unlike a poppy, and yet the seed is the poppy, and the flower is only the seed's way of becoming more seeds. The bright petals are Ember. The dark seed is Umber. Both are necessary. Only one is primary.

So too in your writing: let the downstroke be dark, broad, certain, and few. Let the hairline be light, brief, and subordinate. And between them, with the nib on its diagonal edge, find the arc that holds them in right relation — the meaning between the weights.

All of lettering is this. All of it. Thick lines, thin lines, and the diagonal arc that squares them into sense. There is nothing else to learn, save the patience to learn it ten thousand times over.

Here ends the Parable of Ember & Umber.
The pen that knows its weight will never mistake the juice for the grain.
